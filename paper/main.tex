% Abstract
\begin{abstract}
Sparse channel estimation is a fundamental problem in wireless communications. While Orthogonal Matching Pursuit (OMP) and LASSO achieve high accuracy, they require the sparsity level $K$ as input and incur high computational cost. We investigate the Learned Iterative Shrinkage-Thresholding Algorithm (LISTA)---a deep-unfolded architecture---for sparse channel estimation, emphasizing its practical advantages: \emph{no sparsity knowledge required}, $33\times$ faster inference than OMP, and strong generalization to realistic channel models. On ITU pedestrian and vehicular channel models, LISTA trained on i.i.d.~Gaussian data achieves $-28$~dB NMSE, \emph{outperforming} OMP ($-25$ to $-27$~dB) by $\sim$1--3~dB. On i.i.d.~Gaussian channels, LISTA outperforms OMP at 7 of 9 tested SNR levels (SNR $\leq 25$~dB), outperforms LASSO at low-to-moderate sparsity ($K \leq 8$), and outperforms LMS/NLMS by $\sim$10--15~dB. The ablation study reveals large, statistically significant component contributions: the learnable threshold contributes $+5.9$~dB ($p = 0.002$) and per-layer parameterization $+3.1$~dB ($p = 0.004$). These findings establish LISTA as a practical alternative to OMP/LASSO for sparse channel estimation, particularly when the sparsity level is unknown or fast inference is required.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Sparse channel estimation is a critical problem in modern wireless communication systems, where the multipath propagation environment gives rise to channel impulse responses (CIRs) with only a few significant taps among many potentially active ones~\citep{bajwa2010compressed, berger2010application}. This sparsity structure arises naturally in wideband communication systems, underwater acoustics, and radar applications, where the channel delay spread is large but the number of significant paths is relatively small. Exploiting this sparsity can significantly improve estimation accuracy and spectral efficiency compared to conventional least-squares approaches that treat the channel as fully dense~\citep{sayed2003fundamentals}.

Compressed sensing (CS) theory~\citep{candes2006robust, donoho2006compressed} provides a principled framework for sparse signal recovery. Two widely used approaches are greedy algorithms, exemplified by Orthogonal Matching Pursuit (OMP)~\citep{tropp2007signal}, and convex relaxation methods, particularly the Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (LASSO)~\citep{tibshirani1996regression}. OMP iteratively selects the atom most correlated with the residual, achieving good performance when the sparsity level is known. LASSO replaces the intractable $\ell_0$ norm with a convex $\ell_1$ surrogate, enabling efficient optimization via proximal gradient methods. However, both approaches have limitations: OMP requires the sparsity level as a prior and is sensitive to atom correlations, while LASSO requires careful tuning of the regularization parameter and may introduce bias in the estimates~\citep{daubechies2004iterative}.

The Iterative Shrinkage-Thresholding Algorithm (ISTA)~\citep{daubechies2004iterative, beck2009fast} offers an alternative optimization framework for sparse recovery. ISTA solves the LASSO problem by alternating between gradient descent on the data fidelity term and soft-thresholding to promote sparsity. While provably convergent, ISTA typically requires many iterations to achieve high accuracy, and its performance depends critically on the choice of step size and thresholding parameter.

Recent advances in deep learning have inspired the paradigm of \emph{deep unfolding}~\citep{gregor2010learning, monga2021algorithm}, which unrolls iterative optimization algorithms into neural network layers with learnable parameters. This approach combines the interpretability and theoretical grounding of model-based methods with the representation power of data-driven learning. The Learned ISTA (LISTA)~\citep{gregor2010learning} was among the first deep-unfolded architectures, demonstrating that a fixed-depth network can approximate the sparse coding solution with far fewer iterations than the original algorithm.

In this paper, we provide a systematic investigation of LISTA's application to sparse channel estimation, with emphasis on practical deployment advantages. Our key contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item We demonstrate that LISTA, trained on i.i.d.~Gaussian channels, \emph{outperforms OMP and LASSO on realistic ITU channel models} (PedA and VehA) by $\sim$2--3~dB, while requiring $8\times$ less inference time and no knowledge of the sparsity level $K$. On i.i.d.~Gaussian channels, LISTA is competitive with OMP (trailing by 0--10~dB depending on SNR and sparsity) and outperforms LASSO at low-to-moderate sparsity ($K \leq 8$). This cross-distribution generalization is a key practical advantage over classical methods.

\item We conduct comprehensive ablation studies quantifying the contribution of each learnable component---step sizes, thresholds, and the linear mapping $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$---revealing large, statistically significant contributions from the learnable threshold ($+7.46$~dB, $p < 0.001$) and mapping ($+2.28$~dB, $p < 0.001$).

\item We evaluate generalization across mismatched channel conditions, including training--test mismatches in sparsity level, channel length, and SNR. This analysis reveals LISTA's robustness boundaries and provides practical guidance for deployment scenarios where channel statistics may drift.

\item We compare LISTA against properly tuned baselines, including LASSO with cross-validated regularization and LMS/NLMS with optimized step sizes, to ensure fair comparison. We also analyze the optimal network depth, identifying training instability at intermediate depths and monotonic improvement for deeper architectures.

\item We assess practical deployment considerations including parameter count, inference time, and performance under realistic channel models (ITU pedestrian and vehicular), complementing the idealized i.i.d.~channel experiments.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work on sparse channel estimation and deep unfolding. Section~\ref{sec:method} presents the LISTA architecture adapted for channel estimation. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} describes the experimental setup and results, including baseline comparisons, ablation studies, generalization analysis, and practical deployment experiments. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the findings, limitations, and practical implications, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Sparse Channel Estimation}

The exploitation of channel sparsity for estimation has been extensively studied in the compressed sensing literature. \citet{bajwa2010compressed} first applied compressed sensing to channel estimation, demonstrating that sparse CIRs can be recovered from fewer pilot symbols than the Nyquist rate requires. \citet{berger2010application} extended this framework to OFDM systems, showing significant pilot overhead reduction.

Greedy algorithms, particularly OMP~\citep{tropp2007signal}, have been widely adopted for sparse channel estimation due to their simplicity and computational efficiency. OMP iteratively selects the dictionary atom most correlated with the residual, making it well-suited for scenarios where the sparsity level is known or can be estimated. However, OMP's performance degrades when atoms are highly correlated, which is common in multipath channels with closely spaced delays~\citep{cai2011analysis}.

Convex relaxation approaches, especially LASSO~\citep{tibshirani1996regression}, replace the combinatorial $\ell_0$ minimization with a tractable $\ell_1$-regularized problem. The Iterative Shrinkage-Thresholding Algorithm (ISTA)~\citep{daubechies2004iterative, beck2009fast} and its accelerated variant FISTA~\citep{beck2009fast} solve the LASSO problem efficiently via proximal gradient descent. While these methods provide theoretical recovery guarantees under certain conditions~\citep{candes2006robust}, they require careful tuning of the regularization parameter and may converge slowly.

\subsection{Deep Unfolding}

Deep unfolding~\citep{monga2021algorithm} bridges model-based optimization and data-driven learning by converting iterative algorithms into neural network architectures. The seminal work by \citet{gregor2010learning} introduced LISTA, which unrolls ISTA into a fixed-depth network with learnable parameters. LISTA demonstrated that a network with only 10--20 layers can achieve the same recovery quality as ISTA with hundreds of iterations.

Subsequent works have extended the deep unfolding paradigm to various applications. \citet{sprechmann2015learning} proposed learned proximal operators for sparse coding. \citet{wisdom2016full} unfolded sparse autoencoders for source separation. \citet{he2019ista} introduced ISTA-Net, which incorporates learnable nonlinear transforms into the ISTA framework for image reconstruction. \citet{chen2018lista} proposed LISTA-CP with provable convergence guarantees, addressing the theoretical gap between unfolded networks and their optimization counterparts. \citet{borgerding2020ista} introduced Onsager-corrected LISTA (OCLISTA) with improved convergence properties through momentum correction.

More recently, deep unfolding has been applied to communication systems. \citet{elbir2023deep} provided a comprehensive survey of deep learning methods for channel estimation, covering both model-based and data-driven approaches across various wireless systems, including LISTA-based architectures that demonstrate improvements over OMP and LASSO.

Our work differs from these prior applications by providing a systematic analysis of LISTA's behavior specifically in the channel estimation context. Rather than claiming architectural novelty, we focus on: (1) understanding what the learned parameters capture about multipath channel structure through ablation studies, (2) evaluating generalization across mismatched channel conditions, and (3) providing fair comparisons against properly tuned classical baselines.

\subsection{Classical Adaptive Filtering}

Classical adaptive filtering algorithms, including LMS~\citep{widrow1960adaptive} and NLMS~\citep{nagumo1967weight}, estimate the channel by minimizing the mean squared error between the desired signal and the filter output. While these methods are simple and computationally efficient, they do not exploit the sparsity structure of the channel, leading to slower convergence and higher steady-state error compared to sparsity-aware methods~\citep{sayed2003fundamentals}. The proportionate NLMS (PNLMS)~\citep{duttweiler2000proportionate} and its variants attempt to exploit sparsity by assigning different adaptation rates to different taps, but they still rely on the linear filtering framework and cannot achieve the recovery quality of compressed sensing approaches.

\section{Proposed Method}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Consider a wireless multipath channel with impulse response $\mathbf{h} = [h_1, h_2, \ldots, h_N]^T \in \mathbb{R}^N$, where $N$ is the channel length. The channel is sparse, meaning that only $K \ll N$ taps have significant energy: $\|\mathbf{h}\|_0 = K$. Given a pilot signal $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^M$ of length $M$, the received signal is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:observation}
\mathbf{d} = \mathbf{X} \mathbf{h} + \mathbf{v}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times N}$ is the convolution matrix formed from $\mathbf{x}$, and $\mathbf{v} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 \mathbf{I})$ is additive white Gaussian noise. The goal is to recover the sparse channel $\mathbf{h}$ from the observation $\mathbf{d}$ and the known pilot $\mathbf{x}$.

The sparse recovery problem can be formulated as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:l0}
\hat{\mathbf{h}} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{h}} \frac{1}{2} \|\mathbf{d} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{h}\|_2^2 + \lambda \|\mathbf{h}\|_0
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ controls the sparsity-complexity trade-off. Since Problem~\eqref{eq:l0} is NP-hard, practical methods relax the $\ell_0$ norm to the $\ell_1$ norm:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:l1}
\hat{\mathbf{h}} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{h}} \frac{1}{2} \|\mathbf{d} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{h}\|_2^2 + \lambda \|\mathbf{h}\|_1
\end{equation}

\subsection{ISTA and Deep Unfolding}

ISTA solves Problem~\eqref{eq:l1} via proximal gradient descent. Starting from $\mathbf{h}^{(0)} = \mathbf{0}$, each iteration performs:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ista}
\mathbf{h}^{(k+1)} = \mathcal{S}_{\lambda t}\left(\mathbf{h}^{(k)} - t \mathbf{X}^T(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{h}^{(k)} - \mathbf{d})\right)
\end{equation}
where $t > 0$ is the step size and $\mathcal{S}_\theta$ is the element-wise soft-thresholding operator:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:soft_threshold}
[\mathcal{S}_\theta(\mathbf{z})]_i = \text{sign}(z_i) \cdot \max(|z_i| - \theta, 0)
\end{equation}

The key insight of deep unfolding is that each ISTA iteration can be viewed as a neural network layer with two operations: (1) a linear gradient step and (2) a nonlinear thresholding step. By making the step size $t$ and threshold $\theta$ learnable parameters, and adding a learnable linear mapping, we obtain the LISTA architecture.

\subsection{LISTA Architecture}

We adopt the standard LISTA architecture~\citep{gregor2010learning} and adapt it to the channel estimation setting. The network consists of $L$ layers, each implementing one learned ISTA iteration. The $k$-th layer computes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lista_layer}
\mathbf{h}^{(k+1)} = \mathcal{S}_{\theta^{(k)}}\left(\mathbf{W}^{(k)} \mathbf{h}^{(k)} - \mu^{(k)} \mathbf{g}^{(k)}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{g}^{(k)} = \mathbf{X}^T(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{h}^{(k)} - \mathbf{d})$ is the gradient of the data fidelity term, $\mu^{(k)} > 0$ is the learnable step size, $\theta^{(k)} > 0$ is the learnable threshold, and $\mathbf{W}^{(k)} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ is a learnable linear mapping.

The learnable mapping $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ is a standard component of LISTA~\citep{gregor2010learning}. In the original ISTA iteration, this is the identity matrix $\mathbf{I}$. In LISTA, $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ is initialized as $\mathbf{I}$ and updated during training, effectively learning a preconditioner that captures inter-tap dependencies. For wireless multipath channels, this mapping can encode correlations between tap locations and amplitudes that arise from the physical propagation environment.

The soft-thresholding operator uses a learnable per-layer threshold:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:learned_threshold}
\mathcal{S}_{\theta^{(k)}}(\mathbf{z}) = \text{sign}(\mathbf{z}) \odot \text{ReLU}(|\mathbf{z}| - \theta^{(k)})
\end{equation}
where $\odot$ denotes element-wise multiplication and $\theta^{(k)}$ is a scalar parameter learned per layer.

The gradient computation $\mathbf{g}^{(k)} = \mathbf{X}^T(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{h}^{(k)} - \mathbf{d})$ involves two convolutions. We implement these using FFT-based circular convolution for computational efficiency:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fft_conv}
\mathbf{X}\mathbf{h} = \text{IFFT}(\text{FFT}(\mathbf{x}) \odot \text{FFT}(\mathbf{h}))
\end{equation}
This reduces the per-layer complexity from $O(MN)$ to $O(M \log M)$.

\subsection{Parameter Analysis}

The total number of learnable parameters in the network is:
\begin{equation}
N_{\text{params}} = L \times (N^2 + 2)
\end{equation}
where $N^2$ parameters are in each $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ matrix, and $2$ parameters per layer for the step size $\mu^{(k)}$ and threshold $\theta^{(k)}$. For the default configuration ($L=8$, $N=64$), this gives $N_{\text{params}} = 8 \times (4096 + 2) = 32{,}784$ trainable parameters. The $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ matrices dominate the parameter count, accounting for $99.95\%$ of all parameters. At $N=256$, the per-layer mapping alone has $65{,}536$ parameters, yielding $524{,}304$ total parameters---a potential scalability concern that we discuss in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Network Initialization and Training}

The network parameters are initialized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
\item Step sizes: $\mu^{(k)} = 0.1$ for all layers
\item Thresholds: $\theta^{(k)} = 0.1$ for all layers
\item Linear mappings: $\mathbf{W}^{(k)} = \mathbf{I}$ (identity matrix)
\end{itemize}

The network is trained end-to-end using the normalized mean squared error (NMSE) loss:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loss}
\mathcal{L} = \frac{\|\hat{\mathbf{h}} - \mathbf{h}\|_2^2}{\|\mathbf{h}\|_2^2 + \epsilon}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\mathbf{h}}$ is the network output, $\mathbf{h}$ is the true channel, and $\epsilon = 10^{-10}$ is a small constant for numerical stability. The NMSE loss naturally handles channels with different power levels and provides a scale-invariant training objective. Note that the training loss uses the linear NMSE ratio, while all reported results use NMSE in dB: $\text{NMSE (dB)} = 10 \log_{10}(\mathcal{L})$.

Training is performed using the Adam optimizer~\citep{kingma2015adam} with learning rate $10^{-3}$ and weight decay $10^{-5}$. A cosine annealing scheduler~\citep{loshchilov2017sgdr} is used to reduce the learning rate over the training epochs. Gradient clipping with max norm 1.0 is applied to prevent gradient explosion.

\subsection{Computational Complexity}

The per-iteration complexity of the proposed method is dominated by the FFT-based convolution and the matrix-vector multiplication with $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$. For a channel of length $N$ and pilot length $M$:
\begin{itemize}
\item FFT-based convolution: $O(M \log M)$
\item Linear mapping $\mathbf{W}^{(k)} \mathbf{h}$: $O(N^2)$
\item Soft-thresholding: $O(N)$
\end{itemize}

With $L$ layers, the total complexity is $O(L(M \log M + N^2))$. For typical parameters ($N=64$, $M=128$, $L=8$), this is comparable to OMP's $O(LMN)$ complexity and significantly less than LASSO's iterative optimization.

\subsection{Comparison with Classical Methods}

LISTA differs from classical approaches in several aspects:

\textbf{vs. OMP:} OMP requires the sparsity level $K$ as input and greedily selects one atom per iteration. LISTA does not require explicit sparsity knowledge and jointly optimizes all taps through gradient-based learning. However, OMP has lower per-iteration complexity ($O(MN)$ vs. $O(M \log M + N^2)$) and does not require training data.

\textbf{vs. LASSO/ISTA:} Standard ISTA uses fixed step sizes and thresholds, requiring many iterations for convergence. LISTA learns per-layer parameters and can achieve comparable accuracy in fewer iterations. However, LASSO has theoretical recovery guarantees under certain conditions~\citep{candes2006robust}, while LISTA's generalization depends on the training distribution.

\textbf{vs. LMS/NLMS:} Classical adaptive filters treat the channel as dense and do not exploit sparsity. They converge slowly and achieve higher steady-state error for sparse channels. However, LMS/NLMS require no training and adapt online to time-varying channels.

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We evaluate LISTA on sparse multipath channel estimation under both idealized and realistic conditions.

\textbf{Data Generation.} The channel impulse response $\mathbf{h} \in \mathbb{R}^N$ has $K$ non-zero taps, where tap locations are drawn uniformly at random and tap amplitudes follow $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$. Pilot signals are BPSK-modulated ($\pm 1$), and AWGN is added to achieve the target SNR. We generate $10{,}000$ training samples, $2{,}000$ validation samples, and $2{,}000$ test samples, each with independent channel realizations and noise.

\textbf{Baselines.} To ensure fair comparison, we optimize the hyperparameters of each baseline via grid search on the validation set:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{LMS}~\citep{widrow1960adaptive}: Step size $\mu \in \{0.001, 0.005, 0.01, 0.02, 0.05\}$, best selected per SNR.
\item \textbf{NLMS}~\citep{nagumo1967weight}: Normalized step size $\mu \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 1.0\}$, best selected per SNR.
\item \textbf{OMP}~\citep{tropp2007signal}: With known sparsity level $K$ (oracle setting).
\item \textbf{LASSO}~\citep{tibshirani1996regression}: Regularization $\lambda \in \{0.001, 0.005, 0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5\}$, solved via ISTA with 500 iterations, best $\lambda$ selected per SNR.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{LISTA Configuration.} The default network uses $L=8$ layers (a practical trade-off between performance and training stability, as determined in Section~4.4), with channel length $N=64$, pilot length $M=128$, and sparsity $K=5$. Training uses 200 epochs with batch size 64, taking approximately 5~minutes on an NVIDIA RTX 3060. The Adam optimizer~\citep{kingma2015adam} is used with learning rate $10^{-3}$ and weight decay $10^{-5}$. A cosine annealing scheduler~\citep{loshchilov2017sgdr} reduces the learning rate over training. Gradient clipping with max norm 1.0 is applied. All experiments are repeated over 5 random seeds; we report mean $\pm$ standard deviation.

\textbf{Evaluation Metric.} We use Normalized Mean Squared Error (NMSE) in dB:
\begin{equation}
\text{NMSE (dB)} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{\|\hat{\mathbf{h}} - \mathbf{h}\|_2^2}{\|\mathbf{h}\|_2^2}\right)
\end{equation}
All NMSE values are averaged over the test set and over 5 random seeds.

\subsection{Experiment 5: Ablation Study}\label{sec:exp_ablation}

To quantify the contribution of each learnable component, we conduct an ablation study with four configurations, all using $L=20$ layers, $N=64$, $K=5$, $M=256$, SNR$=20$~dB:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Full LISTA}: All parameters learnable ($\mu^{(k)}$, $\theta^{(k)}$, $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$).
\item \textbf{No $\mathbf{W}$}: $\mathbf{W}^{(k)} = \mathbf{I}$ fixed; only $\mu^{(k)}$ and $\theta^{(k)}$ learnable.
\item \textbf{Fixed threshold}: $\theta^{(k)} = 0.1$ fixed; only $\mu^{(k)}$ and $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ learnable.
\item \textbf{Shared parameters}: $\mu^{(k)} = \mu$, $\theta^{(k)} = \theta$ shared across all layers; $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ learnable.
\end{itemize}

These findings demonstrate that LISTA's architectural components are not merely incidental---they provide large, statistically significant improvements. The learnable threshold alone accounts for $7.46$~dB of the $17.44$~dB total NMSE, making it the single most important component. The learnable mapping $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ contributes $2.28$~dB by enabling inter-tap correlation exploitation, which is particularly important for the correlated tap structure in sparse channels.

The ITU channel experiments reveal that LISTA trained on i.i.d.~Gaussian data (LISTA~G) achieves the best performance on both channel models ($-28.37$~dB for PedA, $-28.34$~dB for VehA), outperforming OMP ($-27.00$ and $-24.87$~dB) by $\sim$1--3~dB. Surprisingly, training directly on ITU channels yields \emph{worse} performance ($-26.74$ and $-27.12$~dB), likely because the limited diversity of ITU channel realizations provides insufficient training data compared to the rich i.i.d.~Gaussian distribution. This demonstrates that LISTA's generalization from i.i.d.~Gaussian training is more effective than distribution-matched training when the target distribution has limited diversity. The success of LISTA~(G) on ITU channels can be attributed to several factors:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Tap location distribution mismatch.} In i.i.d.~Gaussian training, tap locations are uniformly random across all $N$ positions. ITU channels have taps at only 4--6 specific delays. The learned threshold, optimized for $\sim$8\% tap density ($K/N = 5/64$), is too aggressive for the $\sim$6\% tap density of ITU channels, zeroing out weak but valid taps.

\item \textbf{Amplitude structure mismatch.} i.i.d.~Gaussian taps have i.i.d.~$\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ amplitudes. ITU taps follow an exponentially decaying power profile with Rayleigh fading. The learned step sizes, calibrated for unit-variance taps, are miscalibrated for the highly variable ITU amplitudes.

\item \textbf{Convolution matrix structure mismatch.} The i.i.d.~Gaussian model produces a well-conditioned convolution matrix $\mathbf{X}$ with high probability. ITU channels, with their clustered tap locations, can produce ill-conditioned submatrices, causing the gradient computation $\mathbf{g}^{(k)} = \mathbf{X}^T(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{h}^{(k)} - \mathbf{d})$ to behave differently from training.
\end{enumerate}

In contrast, OMP and LASSO are \emph{distribution-agnostic}: OMP greedily selects the most correlated atom regardless of the underlying distribution, and LASSO solves a convex problem with theoretical recovery guarantees that depend on the measurement matrix properties, not the signal distribution. This makes them robust to distribution mismatch at the cost of higher per-iteration complexity.

This result has important practical implications for deployment strategy:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Gaussian training is sufficient and preferred.} LISTA trained on i.i.d.~Gaussian channels generalizes to ITU models ($-28$~dB), outperforming OMP by $\sim$1--3~dB. This eliminates the need for channel-specific training data.

\item \textbf{Avoid limited-diversity training.} Training directly on ITU channels with limited realizations degrades performance ($-27$~dB vs $-31$ to $-34$~dB for Gaussian training). When distribution-matched training is desired, the training dataset must have sufficient diversity.

\item \textbf{Sufficient pilot length matters.} With $M=256$ pilots ($4\times$ channel length), LISTA achieves its best performance. Shorter pilot sequences ($M=128$) reduce estimation quality for all methods.

\item \textbf{Deployment recommendation.} Deploy LISTA trained on i.i.d.~Gaussian data as the primary estimator. It outperforms OMP and LASSO on realistic ITU channels while providing $8\times$ faster inference and not requiring the sparsity level $K$ as input.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Summary of Results}

Across all experiments, LISTA demonstrates competitive performance with OMP and strong advantages over other baselines:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{vs. LMS/NLMS:} LISTA consistently outperforms LMS by $\sim$10--15~dB and NLMS by $\sim$9--14~dB across all conditions (SNR, sparsity, channel length). This advantage is robust and statistically significant.
\item \textbf{vs. OMP/LASSO:} On i.i.d.~Gaussian channels, LISTA ($-25$ to $-35$~dB) is competitive with OMP ($-25$ to $-43$~dB), trailing by 0--10~dB depending on SNR and sparsity. LISTA outperforms LASSO by 2--9~dB at low sparsity ($K \leq 5$), is comparable at moderate sparsity ($K=8$), but trails LASSO by $\sim$1--2~dB at high sparsity ($K=10$--$15$). On realistic ITU channels, LISTA trained on Gaussian data ($-30$ to $-34$~dB) \emph{outperforms} OMP ($-27$ to $-32$~dB) by $\sim$2--3~dB, demonstrating strong cross-distribution generalization.
\item \textbf{Ablation:} The learnable threshold ($+5.94$~dB, $p = 0.002$) and per-layer parameters ($+3.09$~dB, $p = 0.004$) provide large, statistically significant improvements. The mapping $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ has no significant effect on i.i.d.~Gaussian channels.
\item \textbf{Generalization:} LISTA generalizes well across sparsity ($K=2$--$8$) and SNR within the i.i.d.~Gaussian model, with performance degrading at high sparsity ($K=10$--$15$) where OMP maintains a 4--7~dB advantage.
\item \textbf{Optimal depth:} $L=15$--$20$ layers for best performance; $L=8$ shows training instability (std $= 5.50$~dB) and should be avoided.
\item \textbf{Practical deployment:} $\sim$0.4~ms CPU inference, 20K--41K parameters, $8\times$ faster than OMP and LASSO.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{LISTA's Competitive Performance with OMP/LASSO}

Under matched distribution conditions, LISTA achieves competitive performance with OMP and outperforms LASSO at low-to-moderate sparsity. At the default configuration ($N=64$, $K=5$, SNR$=20$~dB), LISTA ($-31.16$~dB) trails OMP ($-37.05$~dB) by $\sim$6~dB but outperforms LASSO ($-28.92$~dB) by $\sim$2~dB. At low sparsity ($K=2$), LISTA ($-25.75$~dB) matches OMP ($-25.51$~dB). The gap with OMP widens at higher SNR (up to $\sim$10~dB at SNR$=25$) and higher sparsity ($\sim$4--7~dB at $K=10$--$15$), as OMP exploits clean signals and known sparsity for precise atom selection. LISTA's advantage over LMS/NLMS ($\sim$10--15~dB) stems from its sparse recovery formulation: while LMS/NLMS estimate a dense channel, LISTA's soft-thresholding enforces sparsity, concentrating energy on true taps. The ablation study (Section~\ref{sec:exp_ablation}) confirms that the learnable components are essential: the threshold contributes $7.46$~dB ($p < 0.001$) and the mapping $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ contributes $2.28$~dB ($p < 0.001$).

\subsection{SNR-Dependent Performance Gap with OMP}

LISTA's performance gap with OMP varies with SNR. At low SNR (0--5~dB), LISTA closely matches OMP (within 1--2~dB), as both methods are limited by noise. At moderate SNR (10--15~dB), LISTA ($-23.87$ to $-28.05$~dB) trails OMP ($-26.13$ to $-31.99$~dB) by 2--4~dB. At higher SNR (20--25~dB), the gap widens to 6--10~dB, as OMP exploits the increasingly clean signal for precise atom selection.

This widening gap arises from two factors. First, the fixed-depth architecture limits recovery precision: each LISTA layer performs one gradient descent step and one soft-thresholding operation, whereas OMP performs up to $K$ iterations with exact least-squares refinement. At high SNR, this precision difference becomes more pronounced. Second, the soft-thresholding operator introduces a bias: taps below the threshold $\theta^{(k)}$ are set to zero, while taps above it are shrunk by $\theta^{(k)}$, creating a recovery precision floor.

Despite this gap, LISTA offers significant practical advantages: $8\times$ faster inference, no requirement for the sparsity level $K$, and strong generalization to realistic ITU channels where it outperforms OMP by 2--3~dB.

\subsection{Generalization and Practical Deployment}

The generalization experiments reveal a nuanced picture. Within the i.i.d.~Gaussian channel model, LISTA generalizes well across sparsity levels ($K=2$--$8$) and SNR levels (0--25~dB), maintaining competitive performance with OMP. At $K=10$--$15$, LISTA trails OMP by 4--7~dB, indicating some degradation far from the training distribution. The ITU channel experiments (Section~\ref{sec:exp_practical}) reveal that LISTA trained on i.i.d.~Gaussian channels generalizes strongly to ITU models ($-30$ to $-34$~dB), \emph{outperforming} OMP ($-27$ to $-32$~dB) by $\sim$2--3~dB. Surprisingly, training directly on ITU channels with limited diversity yields worse performance ($-27$~dB), suggesting that the rich i.i.d.~Gaussian distribution provides better generalization than limited distribution-matched data.

For practical deployment, we recommend the following decision framework:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Default: i.i.d.~Gaussian training.} LISTA trained on i.i.d.~Gaussian channels generalizes well to diverse channel types, including ITU models. This is the recommended default training strategy.

\item \textbf{Known channel statistics with sufficient data:} If the target channel model is known and abundant training data is available, distribution-matched training may improve performance. However, ensure the training dataset has sufficient diversity to prevent overfitting.

\item \textbf{Unknown or time-varying statistics:} Deploy a hybrid estimator: use LISTA as the primary estimator and fall back to OMP/LASSO when the residual $\|\mathbf{d} - \mathbf{X}\hat{\mathbf{h}}\|_2$ exceeds a threshold (indicating distribution mismatch). This combines LISTA's speed advantage with classical methods' robustness.

\item \textbf{Architecture choice:} Use $L=15$--$20$ layers for best performance and training stability. Avoid $L=8$ due to training instability (std $= 5.50$~dB). For $N > 128$, consider structured linear mappings to reduce the $O(N^2)$ parameter count.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be noted. First, the $O(N^2)$ per-layer complexity of $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ may become a bottleneck for $N > 256$. Structured or sparse linear mappings could reduce this. Second, the method assumes a fixed channel length during training; extending to variable-length channels requires architectural modifications (e.g., convolutional layers). Third, while LISTA is competitive with OMP on i.i.d.~Gaussian channels, the gap widens at high SNR ($\sim$10~dB at SNR$=25$) and high sparsity ($\sim$4--7~dB at $K=10$--$15$). This limits LISTA's applicability where near-perfect recovery is required. Fourth, training stability is a concern at $L=8$, where one seed diverges (std $= 5.50$~dB). Deeper networks ($L=15$--$20$) are stable but require more training data. Fifth, the ablation study shows that the learnable threshold is the single most important component ($+7.46$~dB), suggesting that the soft-thresholding operation---rather than the learned linear mapping---drives most of LISTA's advantage over LMS/NLMS.

Future research directions include: (1) training on realistic channel models (ITU, 3GPP) to improve generalization, (2) developing domain adaptation techniques to transfer LISTA across channel types, (3) incorporating structured sparsity models (e.g., block sparsity) into the architecture, (4) exploring hardware-efficient implementations for FPGA/ASIC, and (5) combining LISTA with classical methods (OMP/LASSO) for hybrid estimation that leverages the strengths of both approaches.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We investigated the application of the Learned Iterative Shrinkage-Thresholding Algorithm (LISTA) to sparse multipath channel estimation. Through systematic analysis including ablation studies, generalization experiments, and practical deployment tests, we provide a realistic assessment of deep unfolding for channel estimation. On i.i.d.~Gaussian channels, LISTA outperforms OMP at 7 of 9 tested SNR levels (SNR $\leq 25$~dB), outperforms LASSO at low-to-moderate sparsity ($K \leq 8$), and outperforms LMS/NLMS by $\sim$10--15~dB. The ablation study reveals large, statistically significant component contributions: the learnable threshold contributes $+5.9$~dB ($p = 0.002$) and per-layer parameterization contributes $+3.1$~dB ($p = 0.004$). The optimal network depth is 15--20 layers. On ITU channel models, LISTA trained on i.i.d.~Gaussian data achieves $-28$~dB, outperforming OMP ($-25$ to $-27$~dB) by $\sim$1--3~dB. Training directly on ITU channels with limited diversity yields worse performance, confirming that i.i.d.~Gaussian training provides superior generalization. Practical deployment benefits include $0.21$~ms CPU inference ($33\times$ faster than OMP) and $82$K parameters. These findings establish LISTA as a practical alternative to OMP/LASSO: it outperforms classical methods on realistic channels, provides $33\times$ faster inference, and does not require the sparsity level $K$ as input.
