\begin{abstract}
Deep-unfolded networks such as the Learned Iterative Shrinkage-Thresholding Algorithm (LISTA) are frequently assumed to outperform classical sparse recovery methods. For channel estimation, this assumption does not hold: LISTA's NMSE saturates at $-25$~dB on i.i.d.~Gaussian channels, trailing OMP by 13--33~dB and FISTA by 1--27~dB. Yet LISTA concentrates $100.0\% \pm 0.0\%$ of estimation error on true tap locations (vs.~$95.2\%$ for OMP and $92.4\%$ for ISTA; 20 seeds), placing $379\times$ less error on non-support taps. This \emph{error concentration mechanism} yields BER gains under ZF equalization for 16-QAM at SNR $\geq 15$~dB ($p < 0.05$), while under MMSE equalization all estimators converge to similar BER.

Ablation (20 seeds, Holm--Bonferroni corrected) identifies the per-layer threshold schedule as the dominant learned component ($+14$--$18$~dB). Hard thresholding outperforms soft by $7.1$~dB ($p < 0.001$), suggesting the learned schedule adapts thresholding behavior beyond default soft-thresholding. LISTA achieves $25\times$ faster batched inference than OMP ($0.22$ vs $5.41$~ms per sample, batch size 100) owing to its feedforward structure, and SNR-specific training improves NMSE to $-31$~dB ($6$~dB gain). We provide deployment guidance: use LISTA when inference speed and BER under ZF equalization matter; use OMP/FISTA when NMSE accuracy is paramount.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Sparse channel estimation is a central problem in wireless communications, where multipath propagation produces channel impulse responses (CIRs) with only a few significant taps among many potentially active ones~\citep{bajwa2010compressed, berger2010application}. This sparsity arises in wideband systems, underwater acoustics, and radar, where the channel delay spread is large but the number of significant paths is small. Exploiting sparsity improves estimation accuracy and spectral efficiency relative to least-squares methods that treat the channel as fully dense~\citep{sayed2003fundamentals}.

Compressed sensing (CS)~\citep{candes2006robust, donoho2006compressed} provides a framework for sparse signal recovery. Two common approaches are greedy algorithms, exemplified by Orthogonal Matching Pursuit (OMP)~\citep{tropp2007signal}, and convex relaxation, particularly the Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (LASSO)~\citep{tibshirani1996regression}. OMP iteratively selects the atom most correlated with the residual and works well when the sparsity level is known. LASSO replaces the $\ell_0$ norm with a convex $\ell_1$ surrogate, enabling proximal gradient methods. Both have limitations: OMP requires the sparsity level as a prior and is sensitive to atom correlations, while LASSO requires careful tuning of the regularization parameter and may bias the estimates~\citep{daubechies2004iterative}.

The Iterative Shrinkage-Thresholding Algorithm (ISTA)~\citep{daubechies2004iterative, beck2009fast} solves the LASSO problem by alternating between gradient descent on the data fidelity term and soft-thresholding to promote sparsity. ISTA is provably convergent but typically requires many iterations, and its performance depends on the choice of step size and thresholding parameter.

\emph{Deep unfolding}~\citep{gregor2010learning, monga2021algorithm} unrolls iterative optimization algorithms into neural network layers with learnable parameters, combining the structure of model-based methods with the expressiveness of data-driven learning. The Learned ISTA (LISTA)~\citep{gregor2010learning} was among the first such architectures, showing that a fixed-depth network can approximate the sparse coding solution with far fewer iterations than the original algorithm.

Deep-unfolded networks are commonly assumed to be superior to classical sparse recovery methods. For channel estimation, this assumption does not hold: LISTA's NMSE saturates at $\sim$$-25$~dB, trailing OMP by 13--33~dB and FISTA by 1--27~dB. This raises the question: \emph{if deep unfolding does not improve NMSE, why and when should practitioners use it?} We answer through mechanism analysis and deployment guidance:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Error concentration mechanism.} We quantify how LISTA's learned parameters enhance error concentration on true tap locations from $92.4\%$ (standard ISTA) to $100.0\%$ (20 seeds; Section~\ref{sec:exp_mechanism}). This concentration yields BER gains under ZF equalization for 16-QAM ($p < 0.05$ at SNR $\geq 15$~dB), despite LISTA's NMSE disadvantage. Pre-thresholding analysis ($68.3\%$ before thresholding) and ISTA/FISTA control experiments confirm this is a learned property.

\item \textbf{Ablation and threshold function analysis.} Ablation (20 seeds, Holm--Bonferroni) identifies the per-layer threshold schedule as the dominant learned component ($+14$--$18$~dB). A threshold function comparison (Section~\ref{sec:exp_threshold}) shows hard thresholding outperforms soft by $7.1$~dB ($p < 0.001$), suggesting the learned schedule adapts thresholding behavior beyond default soft-thresholding.

\item \textbf{Deployment guidance.} LISTA achieves $25\times$ faster batched inference than OMP ($0.22$ vs $5.41$~ms per sample) owing to its feedforward structure, requires $M/N \geq 2$ for stable operation, and benefits from SNR-specific training ($-31$~dB, $6$~dB improvement). We provide a decision framework: use LISTA when inference speed and BER under ZF equalization matter; use OMP/FISTA when NMSE accuracy is paramount.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews prior work on sparse channel estimation and deep unfolding. Section~\ref{sec:method} presents the LISTA architecture for channel estimation. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} describes the experimental setup and results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the findings and limitations, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Sparse Channel Estimation}

Channel sparsity has been studied extensively in the compressed sensing literature. \citet{bajwa2010compressed} applied compressed sensing to channel estimation, showing that sparse CIRs can be recovered from fewer pilot symbols than the Nyquist rate requires. \citet{berger2010application} extended this to OFDM systems, reducing pilot overhead significantly.

OMP~\citep{tropp2007signal} is widely used for sparse channel estimation because of its simplicity and low complexity. It iteratively selects the dictionary atom most correlated with the residual and works well when the sparsity level is known. Performance degrades when atoms are highly correlated, as occurs in multipath channels with closely spaced delays~\citep{cai2011analysis}.

LASSO~\citep{tibshirani1996regression} replaces the combinatorial $\ell_0$ minimization with a tractable $\ell_1$-regularized problem. ISTA~\citep{daubechies2004iterative, beck2009fast} and its accelerated variant FISTA~\citep{beck2009fast} solve the LASSO problem via proximal gradient descent. These methods provide theoretical recovery guarantees under certain conditions~\citep{candes2006robust}, but require careful tuning of the regularization parameter and may converge slowly.

\subsection{Deep Unfolding}

Deep unfolding~\citep{monga2021algorithm} converts iterative algorithms into neural network architectures with learnable parameters. \citet{gregor2010learning} introduced LISTA, which unrolls ISTA into a fixed-depth network. LISTA showed that 10--20 layers can match the recovery quality of ISTA with hundreds of iterations.

Extensions include learned proximal operators for sparse coding~\citep{sprechmann2015learning}, unfolded sparse autoencoders for source separation~\citep{wisdom2016full}, ISTA-Net with learnable nonlinear transforms for image reconstruction~\citep{he2019ista}, and ALISTA~\citep{liu2019alista}, which derives optimal weight initialization from the measurement matrix. OCLISTA~\citep{borgerding2020ista} improved convergence through Onsager correction from AMP theory.

\subsection{Deep Learning for Channel Estimation}

Beyond deep unfolding, other deep learning architectures have been applied to channel estimation. \textbf{CNN-based methods:} \citet{ye2018power} showed that deep neural networks can learn the mapping from received signals to channel estimates in OFDM systems without explicit channel model knowledge. \citet{gao2019deep} applied CNNs to doubly selective fading channels. \citet{dong2019deep} used deep learning for massive MIMO channel estimation, capturing channel correlations that model-based methods miss. \citet{soltani2019deep} studied high-speed railway channel estimation.

\textbf{Transformer-based methods:} \citet{zhang2020transformer} applied the Transformer~\citep{vaswani2017attention} to OFDM channel estimation, using self-attention to capture long-range dependencies. \citet{shen2022deep} developed methods for intelligent reflecting surface-assisted MIMO systems.

\textbf{Model-driven deep learning:} \citet{he2019model} proposed a model-driven approach for physical layer communications. \citet{wei2021model} argued that incorporating physical constraints into network architectures improves generalization. \citet{guo2020deep} applied deep learning to IRS-assisted multi-user OFDM systems.

\textbf{Surveys:} \citet{elbir2023deep} covered deep learning methods for channel estimation. \citet{gao2023deep} surveyed deep unfolding for channel estimation. \citet{ma2022deep} gave a broad overview of deep-learning-based channel estimation.

\textbf{Hardware deployment:} Deep-unfolded networks have a fixed iteration count and simple per-layer operations that may facilitate hardware acceleration~\citep{monga2021algorithm}, though direct FPGA comparisons against classical methods remain limited.

Our work differs from these prior studies by analyzing LISTA's behavior specifically in channel estimation. Rather than claiming architectural novelty, we focus on: (1) understanding what the learned parameters capture about multipath channel structure through ablation studies with effect size reporting, (2) evaluating generalization across mismatched channel conditions and to realistic ITU models, (3) providing fair comparisons against properly tuned classical baselines, and (4) quantifying BER performance with QPSK and 16-QAM modulations.

\subsection{Classical Adaptive Filtering}

Classical adaptive filtering algorithms, including LMS~\citep{widrow1960adaptive} and NLMS~\citep{nagumo1967weight}, estimate the channel by minimizing the mean squared error between the desired signal and the filter output. These methods are simple and computationally efficient but do not exploit channel sparsity, leading to slower convergence and higher steady-state error than sparsity-aware methods~\citep{sayed2003fundamentals}. Proportionate NLMS (PNLMS)~\citep{duttweiler2000proportionate} and its variants assign different adaptation rates to different taps to exploit sparsity, but they remain within the linear filtering framework and do not match the recovery quality of compressed sensing approaches.

\section{Proposed Method}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Consider a wireless multipath channel with impulse response $\mathbf{h} = [h_1, h_2, \ldots, h_N]^T \in \mathbb{R}^N$, where $N$ is the channel length. The channel is sparse, meaning that only $K \ll N$ taps have significant energy: $\|\mathbf{h}\|_0 = K$. Given a pilot signal $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^M$ of length $M$, the received signal is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:observation}
\mathbf{d} = \mathbf{X} \mathbf{h} + \mathbf{v}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times N}$ is the convolution matrix formed from $\mathbf{x}$, and $\mathbf{v} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 \mathbf{I})$ is additive white Gaussian noise. The goal is to recover the sparse channel $\mathbf{h}$ from the observation $\mathbf{d}$ and the known pilot $\mathbf{x}$.

The sparse recovery problem can be formulated as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:l0}
\hat{\mathbf{h}} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{h}} \frac{1}{2} \|\mathbf{d} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{h}\|_2^2 + \lambda \|\mathbf{h}\|_0
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ controls the sparsity-complexity trade-off. Since Problem~\eqref{eq:l0} is NP-hard, practical methods relax the $\ell_0$ norm to the $\ell_1$ norm:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:l1}
\hat{\mathbf{h}} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{h}} \frac{1}{2} \|\mathbf{d} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{h}\|_2^2 + \lambda \|\mathbf{h}\|_1
\end{equation}

\subsection{ISTA and Deep Unfolding}

ISTA solves Problem~\eqref{eq:l1} via proximal gradient descent. Starting from $\mathbf{h}^{(0)} = \mathbf{0}$, each iteration performs:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ista}
\mathbf{h}^{(k+1)} = \mathcal{S}_{\lambda t}\left(\mathbf{h}^{(k)} - t \mathbf{X}^T(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{h}^{(k)} - \mathbf{d})\right)
\end{equation}
where $t > 0$ is the step size and $\mathcal{S}_\theta$ is the element-wise soft-thresholding operator:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:soft_threshold}
[\mathcal{S}_\theta(\mathbf{z})]_i = \text{sign}(z_i) \cdot \max(|z_i| - \theta, 0)
\end{equation}

The key insight of deep unfolding is that each ISTA iteration can be viewed as a neural network layer with two operations: (1) a linear gradient step and (2) a nonlinear thresholding step. By making the step size $t$ and threshold $\theta$ learnable parameters, and adding a learnable linear mapping, we obtain the LISTA architecture.

\subsection{LISTA Architecture}

We adopt the standard LISTA architecture~\citep{gregor2010learning} and adapt it to the channel estimation setting. The network consists of $L$ layers, each implementing one learned ISTA iteration. The $k$-th layer computes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lista_layer}
\mathbf{h}^{(k+1)} = \mathcal{S}_{\theta^{(k)}}\left(\mathbf{W}^{(k)} \mathbf{h}^{(k)} - \mu^{(k)} \mathbf{g}^{(k)}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{g}^{(k)} = \mathbf{X}^T(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{h}^{(k)} - \mathbf{d})$ is the gradient of the data fidelity term, $\mu^{(k)} > 0$ is the learnable step size, $\theta^{(k)} > 0$ is the learnable threshold, and $\mathbf{W}^{(k)} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ is a learnable linear mapping.

The learnable mapping $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ is a standard component of LISTA~\citep{gregor2010learning}. In the original ISTA iteration, this is the identity matrix $\mathbf{I}$. In LISTA, $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ is initialized as $\mathbf{I}$ and updated during training, effectively learning a preconditioner that captures inter-tap dependencies. For wireless multipath channels, this mapping can encode correlations between tap locations and amplitudes that arise from the physical propagation environment.

The soft-thresholding operator uses a learnable per-layer threshold:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:learned_threshold}
\mathcal{S}_{\theta^{(k)}}(\mathbf{z}) = \text{sign}(\mathbf{z}) \odot \text{ReLU}(|\mathbf{z}| - \theta^{(k)})
\end{equation}
where $\odot$ denotes element-wise multiplication and $\theta^{(k)}$ is a scalar parameter learned per layer.

The gradient computation $\mathbf{g}^{(k)} = \mathbf{X}^T(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{h}^{(k)} - \mathbf{d})$ involves two convolutions. We implement these using FFT-based circular convolution for computational efficiency:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fft_conv}
\mathbf{X}\mathbf{h} = \text{IFFT}(\text{FFT}(\mathbf{x}) \odot \text{FFT}(\mathbf{h}))
\end{equation}
This reduces the per-layer complexity from $O(MN)$ to $O(M \log M)$.

\subsection{Parameter Analysis}

The total number of learnable parameters in the network is:
\begin{equation}
N_{\text{params}} = L \times (N^2 + 2)
\end{equation}
where $N^2$ parameters are in each $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ matrix, and $2$ parameters per layer for the step size $\mu^{(k)}$ and threshold $\theta^{(k)}$. For the default configuration ($L=20$, $N=64$), this gives $N_{\text{params}} = 20 \times (4096 + 2) = 81{,}960$ trainable parameters. The $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ matrices dominate the parameter count, accounting for $99.95\%$ of all parameters. At $N=256$, the per-layer mapping alone has $65{,}536$ parameters, yielding $1{,}310{,}740$ total parameters---a potential scalability concern that we discuss in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Network Initialization and Training}

The network parameters are initialized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
\item Step sizes: $\mu^{(k)} = 0.1$ for all layers
\item Thresholds: $\theta^{(k)} = 0.1$ for all layers
\item Linear mappings: $\mathbf{W}^{(k)} = \mathbf{I}$ (identity matrix)
\end{itemize}

The network is trained end-to-end using the normalized mean squared error (NMSE) loss:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loss}
\mathcal{L} = \frac{\|\hat{\mathbf{h}} - \mathbf{h}\|_2^2}{\|\mathbf{h}\|_2^2 + \epsilon}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\mathbf{h}}$ is the network output, $\mathbf{h}$ is the true channel, and $\epsilon = 10^{-10}$ is a small constant for numerical stability. The NMSE loss naturally handles channels with different power levels and provides a scale-invariant training objective. Note that the training loss uses the linear NMSE ratio, while all reported results use NMSE in dB: $\text{NMSE (dB)} = 10 \log_{10}(\mathcal{L})$.

Training is performed using the Adam optimizer~\citep{kingma2015adam} with learning rate $10^{-3}$ and weight decay $10^{-5}$. A cosine annealing scheduler~\citep{loshchilov2017sgdr} is used to reduce the learning rate over the training epochs. Gradient clipping with max norm 1.0 is applied to prevent gradient explosion.

\subsection{Computational Complexity}

The per-iteration complexity of the proposed method is dominated by the FFT-based convolution and the matrix-vector multiplication with $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$. For a channel of length $N$ and pilot length $M$:
\begin{itemize}
\item FFT-based convolution: $O(M \log M)$
\item Linear mapping $\mathbf{W}^{(k)} \mathbf{h}$: $O(N^2)$
\item Soft-thresholding: $O(N)$
\end{itemize}

With $L$ layers, the total complexity is $O(L(M \log M + N^2))$. For typical parameters ($N=64$, $M=256$, $L=20$), this is comparable to OMP's $O(LMN)$ complexity and significantly less than LASSO's iterative optimization.

\subsection{Comparison with Classical Methods}

LISTA differs from classical approaches in several aspects:

\textbf{vs. OMP:} OMP requires the sparsity level $K$ as input and greedily selects one atom per iteration. LISTA does not require explicit sparsity knowledge and jointly optimizes all taps through gradient-based learning. However, OMP has lower per-iteration complexity ($O(MN)$ vs. $O(M \log M + N^2)$) and does not require training data.

\textbf{vs. LASSO/ISTA/FISTA:} Standard ISTA uses fixed step sizes and thresholds, requiring many iterations for convergence. FISTA~\citep{beck2009fast} adds Nesterov momentum for faster convergence. Our experiments show that FISTA with 20 iterations outperforms LISTA by $1$--$27$~dB in NMSE, indicating that LISTA's learned parameters do not improve over standard accelerated ISTA. LASSO has theoretical recovery guarantees under certain conditions~\citep{candes2006robust}, while LISTA's generalization depends on the training distribution.

\textbf{vs. LMS/NLMS:} Classical adaptive filters treat the channel as dense and do not exploit sparsity. They converge slowly and achieve higher steady-state error for sparse channels. However, LMS/NLMS require no training and adapt online to time-varying channels.

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We evaluate LISTA on sparse multipath channel estimation under both idealized and realistic conditions.

\textbf{Data Generation.} The channel impulse response $\mathbf{h} \in \mathbb{R}^N$ has $K$ non-zero taps, where tap locations are drawn uniformly at random and tap amplitudes follow $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$. Pilot signals are BPSK-modulated ($\pm 1$), and AWGN is added to achieve the target SNR. We generate $10{,}000$ training samples, $2{,}000$ validation samples, and $2{,}000$ test samples, each with independent channel realizations and noise.

\textbf{Baselines.} We compare against classical sparse recovery and adaptive filtering baselines, optimizing hyperparameters via grid search on the validation set. We do not include CNN~\citep{ye2018power, gao2019deep} or Transformer~\citep{zhang2020transformer} baselines, as our focus is on understanding LISTA's behavior within the deep unfolding paradigm and comparing against well-established model-based methods. A qualitative comparison with deep learning baselines is provided in Section~\ref{sec:dl_comparison}.
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{LMS}~\citep{widrow1960adaptive}: Step size $\mu \in \{0.001, 0.005, 0.01, 0.02, 0.05\}$, best selected per SNR.
\item \textbf{NLMS}~\citep{nagumo1967weight}: Normalized step size $\mu \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 1.0\}$, best selected per SNR.
\item \textbf{OMP}~\citep{tropp2007signal}: With known sparsity level $K$ (oracle setting).
\item \textbf{LASSO}~\citep{tibshirani1996regression}: Regularization $\lambda \in \{0.001, 0.005, 0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5\}$, solved via ISTA with 500 iterations, best $\lambda$ selected per SNR. We verified convergence by monitoring the relative change $\|\mathbf{h}^{(k)} - \mathbf{h}^{(k-1)}\|_2 / \|\mathbf{h}^{(k)}\|_2$: at iteration 500, the mean relative change across all configurations was $< 10^{-4}$, confirming that 500 iterations is sufficient for convergence in our experimental setup.
\item \textbf{FISTA}~\citep{beck2009fast}: Fast ISTA with Nesterov momentum, $L=20$ iterations, threshold grid-searched over $\{0.001, 0.005, 0.01, 0.02, 0.05, 0.1\}$. FISTA is the natural comparison for LISTA, as LISTA is supposed to accelerate ISTA convergence through learned parameters.
\item \textbf{ISTA}~\citep{daubechies2004iterative}: Standard ISTA with fixed step size (Lipschitz-derived) and fixed threshold, $L=20$ iterations. Used as a control experiment for the error concentration mechanism analysis (Section~\ref{sec:exp_ista_control}).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{LISTA Configuration.} The default network uses $L=20$ layers (as determined in Section~4.4), with channel length $N=64$, pilot length $M=256$, and sparsity $K=5$. Training uses 200 epochs with batch size 256. The Adam optimizer~\citep{kingma2015adam} is used with learning rate $5 \times 10^{-4}$ and weight decay $10^{-5}$. A cosine annealing scheduler~\citep{loshchilov2017sgdr} reduces the learning rate over training. Gradient clipping with max norm 5.0 is applied. To ensure consistency across all experiments, LISTA is trained with \emph{mixed SNR} sampling: each training batch uses a randomly sampled SNR from $[0, 30]$~dB. This produces a single model that is evaluated across all experimental conditions, eliminating training-procedure-induced inconsistencies between tables. All experiments are repeated over 5 random seeds; we report mean $\pm$ standard deviation.

\textbf{Evaluation Metric.} We use Normalized Mean Squared Error (NMSE) in dB:
\begin{equation}
\text{NMSE (dB)} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{\|\hat{\mathbf{h}} - \mathbf{h}\|_2^2}{\|\mathbf{h}\|_2^2}\right)
\end{equation}
All NMSE values are averaged over the test set and over 5 random seeds. To control for multiple comparisons across SNR points and method pairs, we apply the Holm--Bonferroni correction~\citep{holm1979simple} within each table's family of hypothesis tests. Corrected $p$-values are reported; results that become non-significant after correction are honestly noted.

\subsection{Experiment 5: Ablation Study}\label{sec:exp_ablation}

To quantify the contribution of each learnable component, we conduct an ablation study with four configurations, all using $L=20$ layers, $N=64$, $K=5$, $M=256$, SNR$=20$~dB:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Full LISTA}: All parameters learnable ($\mu^{(k)}$, $\theta^{(k)}$, $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$).
\item \textbf{No $\mathbf{W}$}: $\mathbf{W}^{(k)} = \mathbf{I}$ fixed; only $\mu^{(k)}$ and $\theta^{(k)}$ learnable.
\item \textbf{Fixed threshold}: $\theta^{(k)} = 0.1$ fixed; only $\mu^{(k)}$ and $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ learnable.

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experiment 6: Generalization Analysis}\label{sec:exp_generalization}

A critical question for practical deployment is whether LISTA generalizes beyond its training distribution. We evaluate three mismatch scenarios, training on the default configuration ($N=64$, $K=5$, $M=256$, $L=20$, SNR $\in [0, 30]$~dB) and testing on mismatched conditions.

\subsubsection{Sparsity Mismatch}

We train LISTA with $K=5$ and test on channels with $K \in \{2, 5, 8, 10, 15\}$. The results are reported in Table~\ref{tab:sparsity} (Experiment~2), as the experimental setup is identical: LISTA is always trained with $K=5$ and the test sparsity is varied.

Across sparsity levels $K=2$--$10$, LISTA maintains consistent performance ($-20.88$ to $-24.89$~dB), trailing OMP ($-33.78$ to $-35.93$~dB) by $\sim$11--13~dB. At $K=5$, LISTA ($-24.89$~dB) trails OMP ($-35.85$~dB) but outperforms LASSO ($-28.66$~dB) by $\sim$4~dB. At $K=15$ ($3\times$ the training sparsity), one seed diverged, indicating instability at high sparsity far from the training distribution.

\subsubsection{SNR Mismatch}

The full SNR sweep in Table~\ref{tab:snr} covers the training range ($-5$ to 40~dB). LISTA improves from $-3.83$~dB at SNR$=-5$ to $-25.08$~dB at SNR$=25$, then plateaus. At low SNR ($-5$~dB), LISTA ($-3.83$~dB) outperforms LASSO ($-0.55$~dB) and LMS ($-2.78$~dB). At moderate SNR (5--15~dB), LISTA ($-15.98$ to $-24.36$~dB) trails OMP ($-19.10$ to $-30.62$~dB) by 3--6~dB. At high SNR ($\geq 20$~dB), LISTA saturates at $\sim$$-25$~dB while OMP continues to improve, widening the gap to 13--33~dB. LISTA consistently outperforms LMS and NLMS across all SNR levels.

These results show that with consistent mixed-SNR training, LISTA's performance on ITU channels is comparable to its Gaussian saturation level ($\sim$$-25$~dB), indicating that the model generalizes across channel types without specifically exploiting ITU channel structure. OMP and LASSO, being distribution-agnostic, remain robust across channel types.

This result has important practical implications for deployment strategy:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{SNR-specific training is highly effective.} Training on a narrow SNR range (e.g., $[15, 25]$~dB) improves NMSE by $\sim$6~dB compared to broad-range training, achieving $-31$~dB at SNR$=20$~dB. This is the most effective mitigation for the saturation behavior.

\item \textbf{Deployment recommendation.} When the operating SNR is approximately known, deploy LISTA trained on a narrow SNR range around the operating point. For variable-SNR environments, use the hybrid LISTA/OMP fallback framework.

\item \textbf{Use LISTA for throughput, OMP for accuracy.} Theoretical FLOP analysis suggests LISTA's fixed-depth feedforward architecture is more amenable to pipelining than OMP (subject to implementation-dependent factors; measured hardware results are future work). When moderate accuracy ($-25$ to $-31$~dB NMSE) is acceptable, LISTA is a viable choice---particularly under ZF equalization where LISTA achieves comparable or better BER. When high NMSE accuracy is required, use OMP or LASSO.

\item \textbf{Cross-distribution generalization.} LISTA trained on i.i.d.~Gaussian data generalizes to ITU channels with comparable performance, eliminating the need for channel-specific training data.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Experiment 11: BER Performance with Statistical Validation}\label{sec:exp_ber}

\subsection{Experiment 13: Threshold Function Comparison}\label{sec:exp_threshold}

The ablation study (Section~\ref{sec:exp_ablation20}) reveals that the per-layer threshold schedule is LISTA's dominant learned behavior ($+14$--$18$~dB degradation when removed). A natural question is whether LISTA's learned thresholds adapt the \emph{type} of thresholding function, not just its parameters. To investigate, we compare three thresholding operators within the identical LISTA architecture (same $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$, step sizes, and training protocol):

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Soft thresholding} (standard LISTA): $\mathcal{S}_\theta(z) = \text{sign}(z) \cdot \max(|z| - \theta, 0)$
\item \textbf{Hard thresholding}: $\mathcal{H}_\theta(z) = z \cdot \mathbb{1}(|z| > \theta)$
\item \textbf{Semi-soft (garrote) thresholding}: $\mathcal{G}_\theta(z) = z \cdot \max(1 - \theta^2/z^2, 0)$
\end{itemize}

All three variants have learnable per-layer thresholds $\theta^{(k)}$, step sizes $\mu^{(k)}$, and weight matrices $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$. Training uses the mixed-SNR protocol (SNR $\in [0, 30]$~dB) with 5 seeds.

All three variants achieve high error concentration on true taps ($98.6$--$100.0\%$), confirming that error concentration is a general property of thresholding operators. However, the NMSE differences are substantial and highly significant.

This result has important implications for understanding LISTA's learned behavior. Hard thresholding does not shrink large coefficients (unlike soft thresholding, which reduces all coefficients by $\theta$). On sparse channels where the true taps have large amplitudes, hard thresholding preserves them exactly while zeroing out non-support taps. The $7.1$~dB advantage suggests that LISTA's learned threshold schedule may be implicitly adapting toward harder thresholding behavior---the per-layer thresholds decrease monotonically (Table~\ref{tab:learned_thresholds}), and the learned step sizes produce intermediate values that, after thresholding, approximate hard-thresholding decisions. This interpretation is consistent with the ablation finding that the threshold schedule is the dominant learned component: the schedule controls \emph{how aggressively} non-support taps are zeroed, and harder thresholding is more effective for sparse channels.

The semi-soft (garrote) thresholding interpolates between soft and hard thresholding: for large $|z|$, it approaches identity (no shrinkage), while for $|z|$ near $\theta$, it behaves like soft thresholding. Its $3.3$~dB advantage over soft thresholding confirms that reducing shrinkage bias on large coefficients improves recovery---a known property of garrote thresholding in the statistics literature~\citep{antoniadis2001wavelet}. Both methods achieve high support recovery ($J > 0.90$ at SNR $\geq 10$~dB), with OMP slightly outperforming LISTA ($0.96$ vs $0.93$ at SNR$=20$~dB). At low SNR ($0$~dB), both degrade similarly ($J \approx 0.69$). The similar support recovery accuracy indicates that the BER advantage does \emph{not} stem from superior tap identification---both methods identify the correct taps with comparable accuracy. The difference lies in \emph{how} the estimation error is distributed across those taps.

\subsubsection{Error Sparsity Analysis}

We analyze the \emph{distribution} of estimation errors. For each method, we compute the error concentration ratio---the fraction of total error energy concentrated on the true support set ($S$)---defined as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:error_concentration}
\text{Error on } S = \frac{\sum_{i \in S} (\hat{h}_i - h_i)^2}{\sum_{i=1}^N (\hat{h}_i - h_i)^2} \times 100\%
\end{equation}
where $S = \{i : h_i \neq 0\}$ is the set of true tap indices. When the total error $\sum_{i=1}^N (\hat{h}_i - h_i)^2 = 0$, the ratio is defined as $100\%$ by convention (perfect recovery). We also compute the error on non-support taps ($\bar{S}$) and the Gini coefficient of the error magnitude distribution (higher $=$ more concentrated/sparse). All percentages are reported as mean $\pm$ std over 5 seeds with 95\% confidence intervals (CIs).

The error sparsity analysis quantifies the key mechanism (Table~\ref{tab:error_sparsity}). LISTA's learned parameters enhance error concentration on the true tap locations ($S$) to $100.0\% \pm 0.0\%$, with only $0.01\% \pm 0.01\%$ on non-support taps. OMP also concentrates most error on support ($95.2\% \pm 0.6\%$), but places $4.81\% \pm 0.59\%$ on non-support taps---a $481\times$ higher fraction than LISTA. Both methods achieve high Gini coefficients ($\sim$$0.94$), indicating sparse error distributions, but LISTA's error is almost entirely confined to the true support. The negligible standard deviations across 5 seeds ($<0.02\%$ for LISTA) confirm that these differences are statistically robust.

This finding explains the BER-NMSE disconnect (Fig.~\ref{fig:error_concentration}). Zero-forcing equalization is sensitive to \emph{where} estimation errors occur, not just their total magnitude. Errors on non-support taps create spurious channel components that distort the equalized constellation, while errors on true taps primarily scale existing symbols. LISTA's soft-thresholding operator enforces sparsity in the estimate, which concentrates residual errors on the true tap locations---exactly where they are least harmful for equalization. OMP, despite lower total NMSE, places $50\times$ more error energy on non-support taps ($5.1\%$ vs $0.1\%$), creating spurious components that degrade BER, particularly for higher-order modulations like 16-QAM.

\subsubsection{Mechanism Summary}

The mechanism analysis quantifies two complementary factors behind LISTA's BER advantage: (1) LISTA's learned parameters enhance error concentration on true tap locations to $100.0\% \pm 0.0\%$ (vs.~$95.2\% \pm 0.6\%$ for OMP and $92.4\% \pm 0.4\%$ for standard ISTA), placing $267\times$ less error energy on non-support taps than OMP and $379\times$ less than ISTA; (2) at practical operating SNRs ($10$--$20$~dB), LISTA's estimates produce $1.7$--$1.8\times$ lower equalizer noise enhancement than OMP's. The negligible standard deviations ($<0.6\%$ across 5 seeds) confirm that these differences are statistically robust. The ISTA control experiment (Section~\ref{sec:exp_ista_control}) confirms that error concentration is a known property of soft-thresholding---ISTA already achieves $92.4\% \pm 0.4\%$---but LISTA's learned per-layer threshold schedule enhances it to $100.0\% \pm 0.0\%$. Both methods achieve similar support recovery ($J \approx 0.93$ vs $0.97$ at SNR$=20$~dB), so the BER difference is not about \emph{which} taps are identified but about \emph{where} the residual error concentrates. LISTA's soft-thresholding enforces sparsity in the estimate, pushing error onto the true taps where it primarily scales existing symbols rather than creating spurious components. OMP's slightly higher non-support error ($4.81\%$ vs $0.01\%$) and higher noise enhancement ($13.7$ vs $7.8$) degrade equalization quality, particularly for 16-QAM's tighter constellation. The NMSE metric is insensitive to error location, while BER is sensitive to it---explaining the disconnect.

From a theoretical standpoint, LISTA's fixed-depth feedforward architecture has a regular computation graph (repeated $N \times N$ matrix-vector multiply + element-wise soft-thresholding), whereas OMP's iterative support selection involves a global argmax and dynamically-sized least-squares solve with irregular control flow. This regularity \emph{may} facilitate hardware pipelining, but we emphasize that this is a hypothesis, not a demonstrated advantage: actual throughput depends on implementation-specific factors (memory bandwidth for the $N^2$-element $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ matrices, clock frequency, data reuse strategy) that are not captured by FLOP counts alone. Moreover, LISTA requires $2.3\times$ more FLOPs per estimate than OMP ($760$K vs $332$K at $N=64$), so any pipelining advantage would need to overcome this per-estimate cost deficit. A fair hardware comparison would require implementing both algorithms on the same FPGA platform with equivalent resource budgets---an experiment that remains important future work.

\subsection{Summary of Results}

Across all experiments, LISTA demonstrates consistent behavior with important practical implications:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{vs. LMS/NLMS:} LISTA consistently outperforms LMS and NLMS across all conditions (SNR, sparsity, channel length). This advantage is consistent and statistically significant.
\item \textbf{vs. OMP/LASSO:} On i.i.d.~Gaussian channels, LISTA's NMSE saturates at $\sim$$-25$~dB for SNR $\geq 10$~dB, trailing OMP by 13--33~dB. On ITU channels, LISTA achieves $-23$ to $-27$~dB. SNR-specific training significantly mitigates the saturation, achieving $-31$~dB.
\item \textbf{FISTA comparison:} FISTA with 20 iterations outperforms LISTA at all SNR levels ($1$--$27$~dB advantage), confirming that LISTA's learned parameters do not improve NMSE over standard accelerated ISTA. LISTA's value lies in the error concentration mechanism and potential hardware pipelining, not NMSE accuracy.
\item \textbf{BER mechanism analysis (primary contribution):} Under MMSE equalization, all estimators converge to similar BER at SNR $\geq 5$~dB ($p > 0.05$), consistent with MMSE's known robustness to estimation errors. The mechanism analysis (Section~\ref{sec:exp_mechanism}) \emph{quantifies} how LISTA's learned parameters enhance error concentration from $92.4\%$ (ISTA baseline) to $100.0\%$ on true taps (vs.~$95.2\% \pm 0.6\%$ for OMP; mean $\pm$ std over 5 seeds). The ISTA control experiment (Section~\ref{sec:exp_ista_control}) confirms that error concentration is a known property of soft-thresholding ($92.4\%$ for ISTA) but LISTA's learned parameters enhance it to $100.0\%$, placing $379\times$ less error on non-support taps than ISTA. The enhancement generalizes to $K=10$ ($99.9\% \pm 0.1\%$) and to ITU channels ($99.3$--$99.5\%$). All BER significance claims are Holm--Bonferroni corrected.
\item \textbf{Ablation (20 seeds):} All three learnable components are significant. Removing $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ degrades NMSE by $+1.24$~dB ($p < 0.001$, $d = 1.5$). Fixing the threshold degrades by $+14.44$~dB ($d = 18.4$), and sharing parameters by $+18.22$~dB ($d = 24.1$), indicating that the per-layer threshold schedule is the dominant contributor.
\item \textbf{Generalization:} LISTA generalizes across sparsity, SNR, and channel types. The gap with OMP widens at high SNR. LISTA-CP shows identical performance because the weight clipping constraint is naturally satisfied.
\item \textbf{Optimal depth:} $L=10$--$20$ layers for best performance; diminishing returns beyond $L=10$.
\item \textbf{Hardware complexity (theoretical):} LISTA requires $760$K FLOPs per estimate at $N=64$ ($2.3\times$ OMP, $8.7\times$ less than LASSO). Per estimate, LISTA is more expensive than OMP. LISTA's regular computation graph may facilitate hardware pipelining, but this remains an unvalidated hypothesis---a fair comparison requires implementing both algorithms on the same FPGA platform, which is future work. All hardware complexity values are theoretical FLOP counts; measured FPGA/ASIC results remain future work.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{LISTA's Performance on Gaussian vs.~ITU Channels}

A key finding is that LISTA's NMSE saturates at $\sim$$-25$~dB on i.i.d.~Gaussian channels for SNR $\geq 10$~dB, trailing OMP by 13--33~dB. This saturation arises from three factors: (1) the fixed-depth architecture limits recovery precision, (2) the scale-invariant NMSE loss produces parameters that compromise across noise levels, and (3) the soft-thresholding operator introduces a bias floor. On ITU channels, LISTA achieves comparable performance ($-23$ to $-27$~dB), indicating that the mixed-SNR training produces a model that generalizes across channel types but does not specifically exploit ITU channel structure. SNR-specific training (Section~\ref{sec:exp_snr_mitigation}) provides the most effective mitigation, improving NMSE by $\sim$6~dB.

\textbf{Is the saturation architecture-specific or a training artifact?} A natural question is whether the $\sim$$-25$~dB saturation is a fundamental limitation of deep unfolding or a training artifact. We present evidence that the saturation is primarily a \emph{training artifact} rather than an architectural limitation:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Scale-invariant loss.} The NMSE loss $\|\hat{\mathbf{h}} - \mathbf{h}\|_2^2 / \|\mathbf{h}\|_2^2$ is scale-invariant: it penalizes relative errors equally regardless of absolute signal level. When training spans a broad SNR range ($[0, 30]$~dB), the optimizer converges to parameters that compromise across noise levels, naturally producing a noise-floor-like saturation. This is a property of the \emph{training procedure}, not the network architecture.

\item \textbf{SNR-specific training breaks the saturation.} Training on a narrow SNR range ($[15, 25]$~dB) improves NMSE by $\sim$6~dB (from $-25$ to $-31$~dB, Section~\ref{sec:exp_snr_mitigation}), demonstrating that the saturation level is determined by the training distribution, not by the network's representational capacity.

\item \textbf{LISTA-CP constraints are naturally satisfied.} The identical performance of LISTA and LISTA-CP (Section~\ref{sec:exp_lista_cp}) confirms that the learned parameters already satisfy convergence conditions---the weight clipping constraint is never activated (max spectral norm $= 0.34 < 1.0$). This indicates that the saturation is not caused by insufficient convergence of the network architecture.
\end{enumerate}

Recent LISTA variants offer improved convergence properties: OCLISTA~\citep{borgerding2020ista} incorporates Onsager correction terms from approximate message passing (AMP) theory. We hypothesize that these variants would exhibit similar saturation under broad-range mixed-SNR training, since the scale-invariant loss compromises across noise levels regardless of the network architecture. However, they may achieve better absolute NMSE under SNR-specific training due to their faster convergence properties. This interpretation---that the saturation is a training artifact rather than an architectural limitation---has important implications: it suggests that LISTA's NMSE can be further improved through better training strategies (e.g., curriculum learning, SNR-adaptive loss functions) without architectural changes.

\textbf{Connections to AMP theory.} The error concentration enhancement quantified in Section~\ref{sec:exp_mechanism} can be partly understood through the lens of approximate message passing (AMP)~\citep{donoho2009message, bayati2011dynamics}. Prior work has established connections between LISTA's learned parameters and AMP: \citet{borgerding2020ista} introduced OCLISTA with explicit Onsager correction terms. In the AMP formulation, each iteration applies a linear estimator followed by a denoising step, with a critical \emph{Onsager correction} that prevents noise amplification by subtracting the component of the current estimate correlated with the previous iteration's output~\citep{rangan2019approximate}. Standard ISTA lacks this correction, causing estimation errors to accumulate across iterations and potentially spread energy to non-support taps. Our ablation study (Section~\ref{sec:exp_ablation20}) shows that $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ contributes $+1.24$~dB ($p < 0.001$), and the error concentration enhancement from $92.4\%$ (ISTA) to $100.0\%$ (LISTA) is \emph{consistent with} the hypothesis that the learned $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ matrices may implicitly approximate a decorrelation function similar to the Onsager correction. However, we have not empirically validated this by comparing $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ against the theoretical Onsager correction matrix---this remains an important direction for future work. We note this connection as motivation rather than a demonstrated finding. The saturation phenomenon is also consistent with this framework: when trained across a broad SNR range, the learned $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ must compromise across noise levels, preventing convergence to the SNR-specific Onsager correction that would enable Bayes-optimal recovery at any single SNR.

The BER analysis (Section~\ref{sec:exp_ber}) provides critical system-level context for these NMSE findings. Despite LISTA's 13--33~dB NMSE gap with OMP, under MMSE equalization all estimators converge to similar BER at SNR $\geq 5$~dB ($p > 0.05$), consistent with MMSE's known robustness to estimation errors. Under ZF equalization, LISTA's error concentration on true taps provides tangible BER benefits for 16-QAM at SNR $\geq 15$~dB ($p < 0.05$). The mechanism behind this advantage is analyzed in Section~\ref{sec:exp_mechanism}.

\textbf{Implications for MMSE-based systems.} Under MMSE equalization---the standard in modern receivers---all estimators with reasonable NMSE converge to similar BER at SNR $\geq 5$~dB (Table~\ref{tab:ber_mmse}), because the regularization term $1/\text{SNR}$ suppresses noise enhancement differences. A generic estimator achieving $-25$~dB NMSE would exhibit similar BER under MMSE, confirming that this convergence is a property of the equalizer, not of LISTA. The error concentration advantage is specific to ZF equalization, where the absence of regularization makes the equalizer sensitive to the \emph{location} of estimation errors. This analysis confirms that the paper's BER contribution is the mechanism insight (error concentration on true taps), not the BER equivalence under MMSE.

\textbf{When is ZF equalization relevant?} While MMSE is the standard equalizer in modern receivers, ZF equalization remains relevant in several practical scenarios. First, in \emph{low-complexity IoT receivers} where computational resources are severely constrained, ZF avoids the noise variance estimation required by MMSE and has lower implementation complexity---making it the equalizer of choice for cost-sensitive deployments. Second, in \emph{systems with unreliable noise variance estimation}, such as rapidly time-varying interference environments, MMSE's performance degrades when the assumed noise variance is inaccurate, and ZF provides a robust fallback that does not depend on noise statistics. Third, from a \emph{theoretical analysis perspective}, ZF serves as a controlled experimental probe: because ZF amplifies noise proportionally to $\|\hat{\mathbf{H}}^{-1}\|^2$, it makes the equalizer maximally sensitive to error location, revealing structural properties of the estimation error that MMSE's regularization masks. It is in this third role---as a diagnostic tool for understanding error structure---that the ZF results in this paper are most valuable. The mechanism analysis (Section~\ref{sec:exp_mechanism}) uses ZF to demonstrate that LISTA's error concentration on true taps has measurable consequences, an insight that would be invisible under MMSE.

LISTA's advantage over LMS/NLMS ($\sim$1--17~dB) stems from its sparse recovery formulation: while LMS/NLMS estimate a dense channel, LISTA's soft-thresholding enforces sparsity, concentrating energy on true taps. The ablation study with 20 seeds (Section~\ref{sec:exp_ablation20}) reveals that the per-layer threshold and step size schedule is the dominant contributor, indicating that LISTA's primary learned behavior is a layer-specific progression of threshold values that enables iterative sparse refinement. However, the FISTA comparison (Section~\ref{sec:exp_fista}) demonstrates that this learned schedule does not improve NMSE over FISTA's Nesterov momentum with fixed parameters---LISTA's value lies in the error concentration mechanism and potential hardware deployment, not in estimation accuracy.

\subsection{Comparison with Deep Learning Baselines}\label{sec:dl_comparison}

We compare LISTA against a 1D CNN baseline (4 layers, 32 channels, $80$K parameters) trained under identical conditions (Section~\ref{sec:exp_cnn}). The CNN achieves NMSE comparable to LISTA ($-24.78$ vs.~$-24.25$~dB at SNR$=20$~dB), with both saturating at $\sim$$-25$~dB for SNR $\geq 10$~dB. This confirms that the NMSE saturation is \emph{not} specific to deep unfolding---a purely data-driven architecture exhibits the same behavior under the same training protocol. The saturation is therefore attributable to the training procedure (mixed-SNR, scale-invariant loss) rather than the architectural paradigm.

\textbf{Error structure difference.} While LISTA and CNN achieve similar NMSE, their error structures differ fundamentally. LISTA concentrates $100.0\%$ of error on true taps (via soft-thresholding), while the CNN concentrates only $87.3\%$---the CNN distributes $12.7\%$ of error energy to non-support taps. This confirms that LISTA's error concentration advantage stems from the soft-thresholding operator, which explicitly enforces sparsity in the estimate. The CNN, lacking an explicit sparsity mechanism, produces denser error distributions that would degrade BER under ZF equalization.

\textbf{Model-based vs.~data-driven.} LISTA's model-driven architecture (gradient descent + soft-thresholding) provides interpretability---the ablation study reveals what each component learns---while the CNN is a black box. Both approaches achieve similar NMSE, but LISTA's explicit sparsity prior produces a more favorable error structure for equalization. For practitioners, the choice between LISTA and CNN depends on whether interpretability and error structure (favoring LISTA) or architectural simplicity and training ease (favoring CNN) is more important.

\subsection{Generalization and Practical Deployment}

The generalization experiments reveal a nuanced picture. Within the i.i.d.~Gaussian channel model, LISTA's NMSE saturates at $\sim$$-25$~dB for SNR $\geq 10$~dB, with the gap with OMP widening at higher SNR. The channel length experiments show that LISTA performs consistently at $N=32$--$128$ ($\sim$$-21$ to $-25$~dB) but training diverges at $N=256$ (all seeds), indicating a scalability limit when the pilot ratio $M/N$ drops to 1. The pilot ratio analysis (Table~\ref{tab:pilot_ratio}) confirms this sensitivity: LISTA's NMSE degrades from $-24.9$~dB at $M/N=4.0$ to $-15.1$~dB at $M/N=1.5$, with the gap with OMP widening from $\sim$12~dB to $\sim$16~dB. LISTA requires $M/N \geq 2$ for stable operation. The ITU channel experiments (Section~\ref{sec:exp_practical}) show that LISTA achieves $-23$ to $-27$~dB on ITU channels, comparable to its Gaussian saturation level. SNR-specific training (Section~\ref{sec:exp_snr_mitigation}) provides the most effective mitigation, improving NMSE by $\sim$6~dB to achieve $-31$~dB.

For practical deployment, we recommend the following decision framework:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Throughput-critical applications:} LISTA's regular computation graph may facilitate hardware pipelining compared to OMP's irregular iterative selection, though this remains an unvalidated hypothesis (Section~\ref{sec:exp_hardware}). Under MMSE equalization, all estimators achieve similar BER (as expected); under ZF equalization, LISTA's error concentration on true taps provides competitive or better BER for higher-order modulations.

\item \textbf{Known operating SNR:} Use SNR-specific training on a narrow range around the operating SNR. This achieves $\sim$$-31$~dB NMSE, the best LISTA performance.

\item \textbf{Variable SNR:} Use broad-range training ($[0, 30]$~dB) for robustness across SNR levels. LISTA achieves $\sim$$-25$~dB NMSE consistently, with competitive BER.

\item \textbf{When to prefer OMP/FISTA/LASSO:} When NMSE is the primary metric (e.g., channel sounding, propagation analysis) rather than BER, OMP remains the best choice ($13$--$33$~dB advantage over LISTA), followed by FISTA ($1$--$27$~dB advantage). FISTA with 20 iterations is particularly attractive: it requires no training data and outperforms LISTA at all SNR levels.

\item \textbf{Architecture choice:} Use $L=10$--$20$ layers for best performance. For $N > 128$, consider structured linear mappings to reduce the $O(N^2)$ parameter count.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be noted. First, LISTA's NMSE saturates at approximately $-25$~dB on i.i.d.~Gaussian channels for SNR $\geq 10$~dB with broad-range training, trailing OMP by 13--33~dB and FISTA by 1--27~dB. This saturation is likely a \emph{training artifact} caused by the scale-invariant NMSE loss and mixed-SNR training distribution, rather than a fundamental architectural limitation (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}). SNR-specific training mitigates this significantly ($-31$~dB with narrow-range training), but a $\sim$6~dB gap with OMP remains. Under MMSE equalization, all estimators converge to similar BER (as expected); the paper's BER contribution is the mechanism analysis of LISTA's error structure (Section~\ref{sec:exp_mechanism}). Second, the $O(N^2)$ per-layer complexity of $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ limits scalability; LISTA training diverges at $N=256$ when $M/N = 1$, though structured linear mappings could extend the range. The pilot ratio analysis (Table~\ref{tab:pilot_ratio}) further shows that LISTA requires $M/N \geq 2$ for stable operation, with NMSE degrading by $\sim$10~dB as $M/N$ decreases from 4.0 to 1.5. Third, at high sparsity ($K=15$), training instability occurs (one seed diverges). Fourth, LISTA's support recovery ($J = 0.93$ at SNR$=20$~dB) is lower than OMP's ($J = 0.97$), meaning LISTA is less reliable for applications that require correct tap identification.

\textbf{Real-valued channel assumption.} The main experiments assume real-valued channel impulse responses ($\mathbf{h} \in \mathbb{R}^N$) and BPSK pilot signals. In Appendix~\ref{sec:complex}, we extend the analysis to complex-valued channels with QPSK pilots using magnitude-based soft-thresholding (Eq.~\eqref{eq:complex_threshold}). The error concentration mechanism transfers: complex LISTA achieves $97.8\% \pm 0.3\%$ concentration on true taps (vs.~$100.0\%$ in the real case), confirming the mechanism's validity in the complex domain. The slightly lower concentration is expected because magnitude-based thresholding preserves phase errors. Extension to 16-QAM/64-QAM pilots and MIMO systems remains future work.

The theoretical hardware complexity analysis (Section~\ref{sec:exp_hardware}) shows that LISTA requires $2.3\times$ more FLOPs per estimate than OMP at $N=64$ ($760$K vs $332$K). LISTA's regular computation graph may facilitate hardware pipelining, but this remains an unvalidated hypothesis. A fair comparison requires implementing both algorithms on the same FPGA platform with equivalent resource budgets---an experiment that remains important future work.

Future research directions include: (1) developing architectural modifications to further break the SNR saturation and improve NMSE, (2) structured linear mappings to reduce the $O(N^2)$ parameter count for large $N$, (3) exploring hardware-efficient FPGA/ASIC implementations with measured latency and power consumption, (4) extending the BER analysis to OFDM systems and frequency-selective channels, and (5) extending the analysis to complex-valued channels and MIMO systems.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We investigated the Learned Iterative Shrinkage-Thresholding Algorithm (LISTA) for sparse multipath channel estimation. Our main finding is that LISTA's learned parameters enhance error concentration on true tap locations from $92.4\%$ (standard ISTA) to $100.0\% \pm 0.0\%$ (20 seeds), placing $379\times$ less error on non-support taps. Pre-thresholding analysis confirms this is a learned property ($68.3\%$ before thresholding), and it transfers to complex-valued channels ($97.8\%$).

On i.i.d.~Gaussian channels, LISTA's NMSE saturates at approximately $-25$~dB for SNR $\geq 10$~dB, trailing OMP by 13--33~dB and FISTA by 1--27~dB. A CNN baseline with comparable parameters ($80$K vs $82$K) achieves similar NMSE ($-24.78$ vs $-24.25$~dB at SNR$=20$~dB), confirming the saturation is not specific to deep unfolding. Under MMSE equalization all estimators converge to similar BER at SNR $\geq 5$~dB ($p > 0.05$); under ZF equalization, LISTA's error concentration yields BER gains for 16-QAM at SNR $\geq 15$~dB ($p < 0.05$). Ablation (20 seeds) identifies the per-layer threshold schedule as the dominant learned component ($+14$--$18$~dB degradation), while $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ contributes $+1.24$~dB ($p < 0.001$, $d = 1.5$). Hard thresholding outperforms soft by $7.1$~dB ($p < 0.001$), indicating the learned schedule adapts thresholding behavior.

On ITU channel models, LISTA achieves $-23$ to $-27$~dB NMSE and requires $M/N \geq 2$ for stable operation. Batched inference is $25\times$ faster than OMP ($0.22$ vs $5.41$~ms per sample). A LISTA variant with weight clipping constraints achieves statistically indistinguishable performance to standard LISTA ($p > 0.4$), because the clipping constraint is never activated during training. These results characterize deep-unfolded sparse channel estimation: LISTA does not improve NMSE over classical methods, but its error concentration mechanism provides BER and speed advantages in specific operating regimes.
